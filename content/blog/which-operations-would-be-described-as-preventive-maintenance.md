Preventive maintenance includes more than just hands-on machine work. It also includes mapping, planning, scheduling, prioritizing, and assigning responsibility.

## Preventive Maintenance Operations on Machines

### Mapping Equipment Assets

The first step is often mapping all the assets and parts that will eventually require attention.

That can include:

- moving parts
- fixed parts
- supporting components
- wear items

After mapping, the next step is prioritization. Priority can be based on:

- how critical the part is
- how often it needs replacement
- how much impact failure has on production

### Maintenance Planning and Systems

Once assets are mapped, they need to be placed into a system. That system could be:

- paper
- a whiteboard
- a spreadsheet
- dedicated maintenance software

Preventive maintenance planning usually falls into three categories:

- time-based
- usage-based
- condition-based

#### Time-Based Preventive Maintenance

Maintenance is performed at fixed intervals, such as every week, month, or year.

This is useful for tasks that should happen regardless of machine condition, such as cleaning or oil changes.

#### Usage-Based Preventive Maintenance

Maintenance is triggered by how much the asset has been used.

This requires some type of tracking system or monitoring to know when thresholds are reached.

#### Condition-Based Preventive Maintenance

Maintenance is triggered by the observed condition of the asset, often using measurements such as:

- vibration
- ampere
- temperature

This can be more precise than simple time-based scheduling.

### Responsibility

Preventive maintenance usually involves several different roles:

- operators
- electricians
- mechanics
- engineers
- managers

Operators often handle daily tasks like cleaning or oiling. Technical specialists handle deeper machine maintenance. Managers usually handle planning, prioritization, and coordination.

## Why Systems Matter

As maintenance complexity increases, simple spreadsheets become less effective.

For larger operations with many machines, a strong CMMS becomes important because it can:

- assign responsibility
- trigger alerts
- schedule work
- create visibility across teams

Preventive maintenance is not just the act of repairing parts. It is the full operational system used to keep equipment working reliably over time.
