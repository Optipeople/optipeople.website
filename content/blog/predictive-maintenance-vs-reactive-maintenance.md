As industrial technology has evolved, maintenance strategies have evolved with it. Traditional reactive maintenance waits until breakdown happens. Predictive maintenance, by contrast, uses data to act before failure occurs.

The key difference between the two is **when** maintenance action is taken.

## Reactive Maintenance

Reactive maintenance, also called a **run-to-failure** strategy, focuses on fixing an asset only after it breaks down.

In this model, maintenance between failures is minimal unless someone decides intervention is necessary.

## Key Points of Reactive Maintenance

### Seen Lower Costs

At first glance, reactive maintenance can appear cheaper because little work is done before a failure occurs.

### Limited Workforce Requirement

Because there is less planning and less preventive work, the workforce requirement can appear smaller.

### Decreased Short-Term Maintenance Cost

Reactive maintenance often aims to get the maximum possible use out of an asset before spending money on it again.

## Example of Reactive Maintenance

A simple example is replacing a car battery only after it fails completely. The action is taken only after the problem has already happened.

This logic is common in many contexts and can make sense in certain situations.

## Benefits of Reactive Maintenance

Reactive maintenance can be appropriate when:

- the asset is inexpensive
- replacement is simple
- downtime has very low consequence
- spare parts are easy to stock

Examples include things like light bulbs or other non-critical, low-cost items.

## Drawbacks of Reactive Maintenance

Reactive maintenance becomes risky when applied to the wrong assets.

It can lead to:

- unscheduled downtime
- more expensive repairs
- lost production
- safety and environmental risk

Short-term savings can quickly be outweighed by the long-term cost of breakdowns.

## When Reactive Maintenance Still Makes Sense

Run-to-failure can be reasonable when the asset has:

- low criticality
- low replacement cost
- low inspection value
- redundancy elsewhere in the system

If one failure does not materially affect production, the strategy may still be appropriate.

## Predictive Maintenance

Predictive maintenance monitors asset condition using data, sensors, and analytical models. Instead of waiting for failure, it identifies patterns that suggest when maintenance should happen.

In simple terms:

- reactive maintenance responds after failure
- predictive maintenance responds before failure

## Why Predictive Maintenance Is Different

With predictive maintenance, the flow of machine data is monitored continuously and used to identify deviations early.

That means teams can:

- plan work before a breakdown
- reduce downtime
- avoid emergency maintenance
- use maintenance resources more effectively

## Predictive Maintenance vs Reactive Maintenance

Reactive maintenance can still make sense for low-value or non-critical assets.

Predictive maintenance is usually the better strategy when:

- failure is expensive
- downtime affects production
- safety matters
- asset performance can be measured through data

## Final Takeaway

Reactive maintenance is simple, but it often becomes expensive when used too broadly. Predictive maintenance takes more structure and data, but it provides a stronger basis for reducing downtime and protecting critical operations.
