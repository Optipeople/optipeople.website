## A Quick Introduction to OEE1 and OEE2

OEE, or **Overall Equipment Effectiveness**, is a measure of manufacturing efficiency. It is calculated by multiplying availability, performance, and quality.

OEE1 and OEE2 are very similar, but the difference lies in what time basis is used in the calculation.

## What Does OEE Mean?

OEE is a way to quantify how well a manufacturing process is performing. A high OEE score indicates that a process is operating smoothly and efficiently. A low score suggests there are areas for improvement.

## How Do You Calculate OEE?

The formula is:

`OEE = Availability x Performance x Quality`

- **Availability**: actual run time divided by planned production time
- **Performance**: actual production rate divided by designed production rate
- **Quality**: good units divided by total units produced

## What's the Difference Between OEE1 and OEE2?

The difference is in the time used as the baseline.

**OEE1** is based on the maximum available time, meaning all 24 hours in a day.

**OEE2** is based on planned production time, meaning only the hours where production is actually expected to run.

### To Specify OEE1 and OEE2

- **OEE1** is based on total available time: `24 hours`, `7 days a week`, `365 days a year`
- **OEE2** is based on planned production time or actual scheduled working hours

## When to Use OEE1

OEE1 is useful when you want to understand overall capacity and the broader improvement potential across a factory or machine.

It helps answer questions such as:

- How much total capacity do we have?
- How much unused potential exists across the operation?

### When Not to Use OEE1

OEE1 is not ideal when you want to improve a specific machine or shift. Because it includes all available time, the number can be influenced by vacations, holidays, and other factors that do not directly reflect operational efficiency.

## When to Use OEE2

OEE2 is better when you want to make actual operational improvements. It focuses on planned production time and filters out irrelevant non-production hours.

That makes it more useful for:

- shift-level improvement
- machine-level analysis
- targeted efficiency work

If you are already working with OEE2, you likely have a stronger operational foundation for improvement work.

To calculate OEE2 properly, you need to know:

- how many shifts you run
- how many hours those shifts work
- how many planned stops you have
- how much actual planned production time remains after those stops
