OEE, or **Overall Equipment Effectiveness**, is a standard used to measure manufacturing productivity. Its main purpose is to help teams understand where production time is being lost and what to improve next.

## Basic Terms of OEE

The article highlights several useful OEE concepts:

- Fully productive time
- Planned production time
- Ideal cycle time
- Run time
- Total count
- Good count
- Quality
- Performance
- Availability

Together, these terms create the framework needed to understand how OEE is calculated and interpreted.

## The Six Big Losses

One of the most important effects of OEE is that it helps point teams toward the **six big losses** in manufacturing.

These losses are grouped under:

- availability losses
- performance losses
- quality losses

OEE does not improve production by itself. It gives teams a way to see where improvement work should focus.

## Lessening Availability Losses

Availability losses typically include:

- unplanned downtime
- planned downtime such as setup and changeovers

Improving availability often means:

- better preventive maintenance
- condition monitoring or predictive maintenance
- stronger downtime tracking
- better setup reduction methods

## Lessening Performance Losses

Performance losses often come from:

- small stoppages
- reduced speed

These losses can be addressed through operator training, better tracking of recurring slowdowns, stronger standard operating procedures, and better machine condition.

## Reducing Quality Losses

Quality losses appear when a process produces defective parts, startup waste, scrap, or rework.

A lower quality score means a larger share of output is not acceptable as finished production.

Typical responses include:

- tracking defect patterns
- monitoring old tools and worn parts
- improving process consistency
- replacing assets that no longer justify the quality losses they create

## The Effect of OEE in Practice

The real effect of OEE is that it turns broad production problems into specific improvement categories.

Instead of asking only, "Why are we underperforming?" teams can ask:

- Is availability the issue?
- Is performance the issue?
- Is quality the issue?

That makes OEE useful as both a measurement method and an operational improvement tool.
